# Source-quality rubric

Weight sources by quality, not by how many say the same thing. Three blog posts
echoing one press release are one source, not three.

## The hierarchy (best to weakest)

1. **Primary sources** — the original study, the company's own filing or
   documentation, the regulator's text, the dataset itself. Quote these directly.
2. **Reputable secondary** — peer-reviewed papers citing primaries, established
   news outlets with named reporters, recognized industry analysts. Good, but
   check what *they* cite.
3. **Aggregators / commentary** — summaries, roundups, opinion pieces. Useful for
   leads and framing; trace their claims back to a primary before relying on them.
4. **Self-interested sources** — vendor blogs, marketing pages, advocacy-org
   reports on their own cause. Usable, but label them as such and corroborate any
   load-bearing claim independently.
5. **Anonymous / unverifiable** — forum posts, unattributed claims, content with
   no author or date. Lead only; never the basis for a stated finding.

## What to check on every source

- **Independence** — does it trace back to the same origin as your other
  sources? Two outlets reprinting one wire story are not two sources.
- **Recency** — note the date. For fast-moving topics (pricing, product,
  policy, markets) anything over ~12 months is suspect; flag it.
- **Primary vs about-primary** — a news article *about* a study is weaker than
  the study. Get the study if a claim hinges on it.
- **Sample / scope** — for data claims, note n, population, and whether it
  generalizes to the reader's situation.
- **Conflict of interest** — who benefits if you believe this? Vendor-authored
  evidence for the vendor's own product gets a caution label.

## Handling disagreement

When two credible sources conflict:

- State both positions and who holds them.
- Say which is **better supported** and **why** — larger sample, more recent,
  more independent, more rigorous method.
- Do **not** average contradictory numbers into a fake midpoint. Report the range
  and your weighting.
- If you genuinely can't adjudicate, say the question is contested and what
  evidence would settle it.

## Confidence wording

Close the brief with one of:

- **High** — multiple independent, recent, high-quality sources agree.
- **Moderate** — decent sourcing but with gaps, staleness, or one notable
  dissent.
- **Low / contested** — thin sourcing, sources conflict, or the best evidence is
  self-interested. Say so plainly and name what would raise confidence.
