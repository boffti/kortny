## The core approach: visual philosophy first

Canvas design produces artifacts that are primarily visual. Text is a design element, not the default communication medium. Before creating anything, articulate a design philosophy — a named aesthetic movement that defines how form, space, color, and composition will communicate.

**Two-step process:**
1. **Design philosophy** (.md) — name the movement, articulate how it expresses itself visually across space/form/color/scale/rhythm/composition
2. **Visual expression** — produce the artifact (PDF or PNG) that embodies the philosophy

The philosophy must guide toward visual communication, not paragraphs. Information lives in design, not explanation.

---

## Naming a visual philosophy

Name the movement in 1–2 words: "Brutalist Joy", "Chromatic Silence", "Metabolist Dreams", "Analog Meditation".

Articulate it in 4–6 paragraphs — concise but complete. Cover how the philosophy manifests through:
- **Space and form**: how elements occupy and define the canvas
- **Color and material**: palette logic, texture, surface quality
- **Scale and rhythm**: how sizes relate, repetition patterns
- **Composition and balance**: where weight sits, how the eye moves
- **Visual hierarchy**: what the viewer sees first, second, last

**Critical constraint**: the philosophy must emphasize craftsmanship — the final work should appear as though it took considerable time, was labored over with care, and comes from deep expertise. The philosophy also needs creative room — it specifies aesthetic direction, not every element, so the execution can make interpretive choices at a high level.

## Design philosophy examples

**"Concrete Poetry"**
Communication through monumental form and bold geometry. Massive color blocks, sculptural typography (huge single words, tiny labels), Brutalist spatial divisions. Ideas expressed through visual weight and spatial tension, not explanation. Text as rare, powerful gesture — never paragraphs, only essential words integrated into the visual architecture.

**"Chromatic Language"**
Color as the primary information system. Geometric precision where color zones create meaning. Typography minimal — small sans-serif labels letting chromatic fields communicate. Information encoded spatially and chromatically. Words only to anchor what color already shows.

**"Analog Meditation"**
Quiet visual contemplation through texture and breathing room. Paper grain, ink bleeds, vast negative space. Photography and illustration dominate. Typography whispered — small, restrained, serving the visual. Japanese photobook aesthetic.

**"Organic Systems"**
Natural clustering and modular growth patterns. Rounded forms, organic arrangements, color from nature through architecture. Information shown through visual diagrams, spatial relationships, iconography. Text only for key labels floating in space.

*(These are condensed examples. The actual design philosophy should be 4–6 substantial paragraphs.)*

## Deducing the subtle reference

Before creating the canvas, identify the subtle conceptual thread from the original request. The reference is embedded within the art — not always literal, always sophisticated. Someone familiar with the subject should feel it intuitively; others simply experience a masterful abstract composition.

The design philosophy provides the aesthetic language. The deduced topic provides the soul — the quiet conceptual DNA woven into form, color, and composition.

---

## Creating the canvas

With philosophy and conceptual framework established:

- Use the design philosophy as the literal foundation — every decision traces back to it
- Create one highly visual, design-forward PDF or PNG (unless more pages are requested)
- Use repeating patterns and precise shapes to build meaning through accumulation
- Typography: minimal, integrated as visual element, design-forward — never lengthy blocks
- Sparse clinical labels and reference markers can suggest systematic observation
- Anchor with simple phrase(s) positioned subtly; limited, intentional color palette
- Text and graphics must stay within canvas boundaries with proper margins — nothing overlapping, nothing cut off

**Font usage**: Fonts live in `../assets/fonts/` (relative to the canvas-fonts dir in the original skill). When selecting fonts for a canvas, choose faces that serve the philosophy. Script or handwritten faces for organic themes; geometric sans for minimalist or tech; serif for editorial or classical. The font should feel like it was chosen for this specific work.

---

## Multi-page option

When more pages are requested: treat the first page as one page in a coffee table book. Each additional page is a unique twist on the philosophy — same aesthetic DNA, distinctly different expression. Pages should tell a story in a restrained, tasteful way.

---

## Refinement pass (always do this)

After the first version: do not add more graphics. Instead, refine what exists:
- Is the composition more cohesive?
- Does every element serve the philosophy?
- Is spacing flawless and every detail deliberate?

The question to ask before any change: "How can I make what's already here more of a piece of art?" rather than "What can I add?"

---

## Essential principles

- **Visual philosophy**: an aesthetic worldview, not a layout template
- **Minimal text**: sparse, essential-only, integrated as visual element
- **Spatial expression**: ideas communicate through space, form, color, composition
- **Artistic freedom**: the philosophy guides, not constrains, execution
- **Expert craftsmanship**: the final work must look meticulously considered — not generated
