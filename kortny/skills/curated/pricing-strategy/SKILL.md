## Goal

Deliver a pricing recommendation grounded in value, competitive context, and the team's growth model — not just a number.

## Steps

1. **Clarify the context** — what stage is this? (New pricing from scratch, pricing a new tier, adjusting existing prices, pricing a new feature.) What's the growth motion (PLG, sales-led, hybrid)?
2. **Establish the value metric** — what does the customer pay for that scales with the value they receive? (Per seat, per usage unit, per outcome.) This is the most important decision. See `references/pricing-models.md`.
3. **Research competitive pricing** — what do the top 3 competitors charge? Use web search (pricing pages) and note the research date. Identify where your product is priced relative to the market.
4. **Use workspace facts** — pull known product positioning, ICP, and customer segments from Kortny's knowledge graph to ground the recommendation.
5. **Draft the recommendation** — state the recommended model, tiers (if applicable), price points with rationale, and what's included/excluded per tier.
6. **Address willingness to pay** — if pricing research data exists (surveys, win/loss analysis), incorporate it. If not, flag this as an assumption.
7. **Post to Slack** — post summary recommendation. Offer a canvas for the full analysis.

## Rules

- Never recommend a price without stating the value metric it's tied to.
- Competitive pricing is context, not the answer. Price to value; check competitors to validate.
- Price anchoring: lead with the highest tier on pricing pages (it makes the middle tier feel reasonable).
- Freemium only makes sense if the free tier demonstrates core value AND creates a natural upgrade trigger.
- "Charge more" is often the right answer for B2B — underprice signals low quality. Name this explicitly if it applies.
- Do not fabricate competitor pricing — note "price not publicly listed" for sales-gated products.
